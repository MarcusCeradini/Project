Gallium (Ga)

Gallium (Ga), with atomic number 31, is a soft, silvery metal that is remarkable for its low melting point—about 29.8°C (85.6°F)—which allows it to melt in the palm of your hand. Discovered in 1875 by French chemist Paul-Émile Lecoq de Boisbaudran, gallium was named after “Gallia,” the Latin word for France. Though rare in nature and not found in elemental form, gallium is typically extracted as a byproduct of refining aluminum and zinc ores. Its unique physical and chemical properties make gallium a key material in the field of electronics and optoelectronics. It is widely used in the production of gallium arsenide (GaAs) and gallium nitride (GaN), semiconductors that are vital for high-speed and high-frequency electronics such as smartphones, LEDs, solar panels, satellite communication systems, and laser diodes. These compounds are preferred over traditional silicon in certain applications because they offer faster electron mobility and superior performance in harsh conditions. Gallium’s non-toxic and non-reactive nature makes it useful in medical thermometers as an alternative to mercury and in radiopharmaceuticals for cancer diagnostics and treatment, particularly in gallium scans for detecting inflammation and tumors. Additionally, gallium alloys are used in cooling systems, such as in high-performance computing and aerospace, because of their excellent thermal conductivity and ability to remain liquid over a broad temperature range. Despite its many uses, gallium is still relatively rare and expensive, which limits its mass adoption in some areas. Nevertheless, its role in advancing cutting-edge technology—from green energy and telecommunications to medical imaging and space tech—cements gallium as a fascinating and increasingly valuable element in the modern scientific and industrial landscape.
