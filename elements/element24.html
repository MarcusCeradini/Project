Chromium (K)

Chromium (Cr), a hard, steel-gray transition metal with atomic number 24, was discovered in 1797 by French chemist Louis-Nicolas Vauquelin, who named it after the Greek word chroma due to the vivid colors of its compounds. Primarily found in chromite ores, chromium is most famous for its role in producing stainless steel, where it is added to make the alloy highly corrosion-resistant and durable. This property makes chromium essential in industries ranging from construction and food processing to medical instruments and kitchenware. Additionally, chromium is used in chrome plating to provide a shiny, protective finish for automotive parts and hardware, and in the production of vibrant pigments, such as chromium oxide for green colors and chromium sulfate for yellow, found in ceramics, paints, and dyes. Chromium also plays a role in leather tanning, preserving and dyeing animal hides.
