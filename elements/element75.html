Rhenium (Re)

Rhenium, element number 75 with the symbol Re, is a rare, silvery-white, and exceptionally dense transition metal discovered in 1925 by Walter Noddack, Ida Tacke, and Otto Berg, who named it after the Rhine River region where it was first commercially isolated from molybdenite. 1 Notably, rhenium has one of the highest melting points of all elements, exceeded only by tungsten and carbon, and it retains its strength at high temperatures, making it crucial for high-performance applications. 2 Primarily obtained as a byproduct of molybdenum and copper mining, rhenium is highly valued for its use in superalloys for jet engine components and gas turbines, where it enhances strength and creep resistance at extreme temperatures, allowing for greater fuel efficiency. Additionally, rhenium serves as an important catalyst in petroleum refining, particularly in the production of high-octane gasoline, and finds applications in electrical contacts, thermocouples for high-temperature measurements, and filaments in mass spectrographs and flash photography. Its resistance to wear and corrosion, combined with its high melting point, also makes it useful in specialized alloys for aerospace, high-strength tools, and certain medical applications, including radioactive isotopes for cancer treatment, highlighting its indispensable role in various advanced technologies despite its scarcity in the Earth's crust.
